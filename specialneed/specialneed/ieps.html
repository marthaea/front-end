Lesson 5: Developing and Implementing IEPs

Supporting students through personalized learning plans

An Individualized Education Program (IEP) is more than just paperwork—it’s a personalized roadmap for student success. In this lesson, you’ll learn what an IEP is, who is involved, and how it helps students with special needs thrive in the classroom.

What’s an IEP?

An IEP is a legal document that outlines:

  • A student’s learning strengths and challenges
  • Annual goals tailored to their needs
  • Services and accommodations they’ll receive

It’s specifically designed for students who qualify under special education laws.

Teamwork Is Key

IEPs are created by a collaborative team committed to student success.

Who Creates the IEP?

The IEP team includes:

  • Classroom teachers
  • Special educators
  • Parents or guardians
  • School psychologists
  • Sometimes the student, depending on age

Everyone’s voice matters—parents are equal partners in this process.

IEP Contents:

  • Present levels of performance
  • SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound)
  • Accommodations (e.g., extra time, visual supports)
  • Services (e.g., speech therapy, resource support)

How Do Teachers Use the IEP?

General education teachers play a crucial role in implementing IEPs:

  • Keep a copy of the IEP accessible
  • Review goals regularly
  • Collaborate with support staff

IEPs are not optional—they are a part of your teaching responsibility.

Consistency Makes a Difference

Consistency in accommodations builds trust and supports student progress.
