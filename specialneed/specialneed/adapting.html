Lesson 3: Adapting Instruction for Special Needs

Small adjustments that make a big difference

Every student learns differently and for students with special needs, small adjustments can make a big difference. This lesson shows how to modify teaching strategies so all learners can access the curriculum and succeed.

Flexibility is Key

Rigid teaching doesn't serve diverse classrooms. Instead, think:

  • Can I give instructions in more than one way?
  • Could students show their learning through drawing, speaking, or hands-on work?

Even small changes, like offering extra wait time or using visuals, can boost confidence and comprehension.

Modify, Don't Simplify

Adapting instruction doesn't mean making it easier, it means making it accessible.

Examples of Adaptations:

  • Breaking big tasks into steps
  • Providing sentence starters
  • Offering audio versions of text

These supports help students engage with the same content as their peers, just in a way that works for them.

Use Multi-Sensory Techniques

Some students need to touch, move, or hear to learn best.

  • Use manipulatives in math
  • Add music to lessons
  • Encourage movement during breaks or transitions

Collaborate and Reflect

Work with support staff and families to tailor strategies. What worked today? What didn't? Adapting instruction is ongoing; it's about meeting students where they are and helping them grow.

Adapting instruction is only one part of the puzzle. Creating a truly inclusive environment means ensuring that every student feels safe, valued, and part of the classroom community.
